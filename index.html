Three thousand years

Older than the fan.
Older than the wall socket.

Vetiver isn't a wellness trend that found a heritage story. It's a heritage that the wellness industry is only now catching up to.

Sangam era · c. 300 BCE

The root that is dug up

Old Tamil literature records vetiver's medicinal use. The name itself — vettiver — means exactly that: the root you dig up.

c. 100 BCE

Ushira, in the Ayurvedic canon

Classified as sheeta virya — cooling in potency. Prescribed against fever and the burning of the skin.

16th century

Khus ki tatti

Woven root screens hung in doorways and wetted through the afternoon. "When water is thrown on it," a historian of the period wrote, "winter seems to arrive in the midst of summer."

Then, and still

Garlands for the deity

Vetiver garlands adorn the murti of Nataraja in temples across the south, and are among the offerings most favoured by Ganesha.

Today · Ottanchatharam

The same root, the same hands

Harvested from independent farmers, cleaned, sun-dried, and hand-tied by rural women artisans. Nothing about the method has needed improving.

Where it comes from

We buy the whole harvest.

Most craft brands buy a sack of roots from a trader and call it sourcing. We work the other end of the chain: we take the crop off the farmer's field — more than three hundred tonnes of it — and find a use for every part.

01 · FIELD

Independent farmers

Vetiver is bought directly from smallholders around Dindigul at a committed price, before harvest. No trader sits in the middle taking the margin.

How to use it

Hang it. Mist it. That's all.

There is no ritual to learn and nothing to replace. The roots do the work; water wakes them up.

STEP 01

Hang it where air moves

An entrance, a bedroom door, the pooja room, a car mirror, a desk. Airflow is what carries the aroma — a sealed cupboard will do nothing.

STEP 02

Mist it with water

A light spray, not a soak. The roots release their cool, earthy scent as the water evaporates. Repeat whenever the room needs it.

STEP 03

Let it breathe

Keep it out of standing sun and let air circulate. Cared for this way, a single garland stays fragrant for up to 36 months.

Questions

Before you buy

What is a vetiver garland actually for?

You hang it — at an entrance, in a bedroom, in the pooja room, in a car or at a workspace. Misted with water it releases a cool, earthy aroma and freshens the air. Indian homes and temples have used vetiver root this way for over 3,000 years.

Is vetiver the same thing as khus?

Yes. Vetiver (Chrysopogon zizanioides) is called khus in North India and vettiver in Tamil — the word means "the root that is dug up". It's the same plant used in khus curtains, desert-cooler pads and Ayurvedic cooling preparations.

How long does the aroma really last?

Up to 36 months. It isn't a constant perfume — the scent is strongest right after you mist it, which is the traditional method and reactivates the fragrance each time.

How do I care for it?

Mist lightly, don't soak. Let air move around it. Keep it out of continuous direct sunlight. There's nothing else to do and nothing to refill.

Where do you ship, and how long does it take?

Across India, free. Orders leave Dindigul, Tamil Nadu within 2 working days and usually arrive in 3–7 days depending on your pin code. You'll get a tracking link by SMS and email.

Is it safe around children and pets?

It's dried grass root and cotton cord — nothing is added to it, no chemicals, no synthetic fragrance, no plastic. Hang it out of reach of small children as you would any hanging object.
